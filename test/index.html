I disagree that having your own room is unnecessary. I believe personal space is crucial for several reasons.

Firstly, privacy allows for better concentration when studying. For example, when I need to prepare for exams, having my own room means I can focus without interruptions from family conversations or television noise.

Secondly, personal space supports mental health and independence. Teenagers especially need a place where they can express themselves freely, whether through decorating, listening to music, or simply having time alone to process their thoughts.

I partially agree that there's too much noise everywhere, but the situation varies significantly by location.

In urban environments, noise pollution is indeed a serious problem. For instance, at school, the constant chatter in hallways, construction work nearby, and traffic outside can make concentration difficult during lessons. Similarly, shopping centers bombard us with background music, announcements, and the general buzz of crowds, creating a sensory overload.

At home, modern life brings various noise sources: television, household appliances, neighbors, and street traffic. This constant auditory stimulation can increase stress levels and affect sleep quality.

However, the statement "everywhere" is too absolute. Many spaces remain relatively quiet: libraries, museums, nature reserves, and religious buildings. These sanctuaries provide necessary relief from urban noise pollution.

Furthermore, some noise is essential for communication and social interaction. The challenge lies in finding the right balance between necessary sounds and excessive noise that genuinely disrupts our well-being.

I strongly agree that sound levels at concerts and discos pose genuine health risks.

From a medical perspective, prolonged exposure to sounds above 85 decibels can cause permanent hearing damage. Concert venues often exceed 100-110 decibels, which is comparable to standing near a jet engine. This can lead to tinnitus (ringing in the ears), hearing loss, and in extreme cases, permanent deafness.

Moreover, many young people are unaware of these risks. I've witnessed friends leaving concerts with temporary hearing impairment, joking about "ringing ears" without realizing the potential long-term consequences.

While some argue that attendance is voluntary, this perspective oversimplifies the issue. Many venues could reduce sound levels without significantly affecting the musical experience, and better awareness could help people make informed decisions about ear protection.

My friend Jake disagrees, arguing that loud music is integral to the concert experience and that people should simply wear earplugs if concerned. However, I believe venues have a responsibility to prioritize patron safety while still delivering an engaging experience.

I believe mobile phones present both dangers and disturbances, though the severity depends largely on usage patterns.

Regarding dangers, phone addiction is a genuine concern. Studies show that excessive screen time can lead to anxiety, depression, and sleep disorders. For example, many teenagers check their phones over 150 times daily, creating constant stress from social media notifications and the fear of missing out (FOMO).

Phones also disturb social interactions. I've observed conversations interrupted by notifications, families dining in silence while scrolling, and students struggling to concentrate in class due to the urge to check messages.

However, phones aren't inherently problematic‚Äîthe issue lies in how we use them. When used mindfully, they're powerful tools for communication, learning, and emergency safety.

My friend Lisa argues that self-control is the key factor, and she manages her phone usage through apps that limit screen time. While I agree personal responsibility matters, I think the addictive design of social media platforms makes self-control increasingly difficult, especially for young people.

üí° Sample Answer Grade: A

History serves as humanity's collective memory, offering invaluable lessons for navigating present challenges.

Primarily, history teaches us about the consequences of actions and decisions. The rise and fall of civilizations show us how factors like environmental degradation, economic inequality, and political corruption can destabilize societies. For example, the collapse of Easter Island civilization demonstrates how unsustainable resource use can lead to societal breakdown.

Additionally, history reveals patterns in human behavior and social movements. Understanding how past societies addressed challenges like pandemics, economic crises, or social injustice provides frameworks for contemporary solutions.

Some argue that historical circumstances were so different that past lessons don't apply to modern situations. They contend that technology and globalization have fundamentally changed human society.

However, while contexts change, fundamental human nature and social dynamics remain remarkably consistent. The same emotions, motivations, and social structures that drove historical events continue to influence modern society, making historical knowledge highly relevant for understanding and shaping our future.
